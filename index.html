Visualization #1

Race and Income Distribution Overview

This bar chart visualizes the percentage of each racial group in the dataset making over $50,000 per year. As can be seen, the racial group that has the most individuals making over $50,000 per year, on average, is the Asian-Pacific-Islander group, with over 28 percent earning so. Closely following this group are White Americans, with over 26 percent earning above the threshold. In contrast, Black and American-Indian-Eskimo populations have significantly lower percentages at 11.6% and 9.9%, respectively. This difference helps to display a clear disparity between earnings for different racial groups in the United States in 1999.

Visualization #2

Analysis of Connection Between Hours Worked and Income

This line chart shows a clear positive relationship between the percentage of individuals earning more than $50,000 and the number of hours worked per week for that given group. For lower hours, especially below 30 hours per week, the proportion of high earners tends to be low, generally below 10 percent. In contrast, as the weekly hours worked increase to above 35, the percentage of individuals earning above $50,000 rises significantly, displaying that working longer hours is strongly associated with higher income levels. While this trend does tend to fluctuate between 40 and 60 hours worked per week, it does continue upward overall. This graph suggests that higher-income individuals are more likely to work longer hours, displaying a potential connection between time commitment to work and earning potential.
